Course Description

General introduction of modern programming languages and paradigms, including imperative programming, object-oriented programming, meta programming, concurrent programming and functional programming. The course will be supplemented by coding assignments using popular programming languages such as C++11, Python, and Rust.

Note that the course description on the academic calendar is not up-to-date.

The midterm and the final exam will be closed book exams. No calculators or cheat sheets are allowed for either exam.

*There will be 12 quizzes, but you only need to complete 10 out of the 12 to receive full marks. These quizzes are intended as exercises and thus will only be checked for completion and not for correctness by the TAs. The TAs will provide the answer to each quiz at the end of the tutorial.

There will strictly be no extensions for assignments. It is your responsibility to give yourself enough time to properly submit your assignment.

For any remarking requests, contact the course instructor directly. You may not submit a remarking request later than two weeks after the assignment or exam is returned.

Policy on Cheating

Plagiarism and cheating WILL NOT be tolerated under any circumstances. Cheating includes, but is not limited to, generating expected output in an attempt to fool the automated marking script, or using unauthorized aids in assignments and exams. It is also an offense to allow a copy of your work to be submitted by another person for credit, whether with or without your knowledge. As such, it is your responsibility to protect your work from being copied by others.

All assignments submitted for credit in this course will be compared pairwise to attempt to identify cases of collusion, copying, and similar offenses. The comparison is performed by a sophisticated program that is capable of detecting similar programs even if considerable effort has been taken to conceal their similarity.

Any work submitted for credit that is not the work of the person(s) submitting it will be treated as an offense under the Code of Academic Discipline of the University. The Code of Academic Discipline will be rigidly enforced in this course. Penalties can range from grade penalties in the course to suspension from the university. The Dean, as advised by the instructors and the Departmental Chair, determines the penalty for each case.

For more details, please refer to the Academic Integrity web site at the University.

The suggested textbooks for this course are:

  • The C++ Programming Language (4th Edition) by Bjarne Stroustrup
  • Thinking Python: How to Think Like a Computer Scientist
  • The Rust Programming Language (2018 Edition)
  • Concepts of Programming Languages (11th Edition) by Robert W. Sebesta, 2015.

None of the suggested textbooks are mandatory. Midterm and final exam questions will be sourced exclusively from the lecture notes.
